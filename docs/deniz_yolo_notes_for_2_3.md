One of the primary objectives of this project is to develop a robust computer vision system capable of identifying and tracking cotton bolls in real-time. The system is required to maintain a unique Identification Number (ID) for each cotton boll, even under challenging conditions such as 3D rotation, changes in distance, and temporary occlusion.
The project utilized the YOLO (You Only Look Once) architecture for object detection and compared distinct tracking algorithms to achieve stability on an NVIDIA RTX 3050 GPU.
2.3.1 Data Collection
The foundation of any machine learning model is its dataset. To ensure the model could recognize cotton bolls in various orientations, video data was collected using a standard cellphone camera, for demo purposes. Also captured images from our 3D cotton model and edited to create unripe cotton bolls. The dataset is chosen to have diverse angles, variable lighting, and motion blur for robustness. In order to achieve robustness, the cotton was recorded rotating 360 degrees to capture all sides. Recordings were made under different lighting conditions to simulate real-world environments and the dataset included frames with moving cotton to train the model to recognize non-static objects.
2.3.1 Data Annotation (Labeling)
The collected video frames were processed and labeled using Roboflow, a computer vision platform. To label an image, we manually drew rectangular bounding boxes around the visible individual cotton bolls in each frame. 3 classes, "cotton-boll" and “cotton-boll-cluster”, and “unripe-cotton-boll”  were defined. The cotton boll bounding boxes were labeled as the class of “cotton-boll” and the group of cottons was labeled as “cotton-boll-cluster” . An average of 16 bounding boxes of individual cotton, and a single bounding box of a cluster existed in a single image, with a total of 100 images. Also improved dataset with 35 more images including an average of 4 unripe cotton boll and 6 individual cotton boll bounding boxes. These images were resized and normalized within Roboflow to ensure consistent input for the neural network. To enlarge our dataset, we applied augmentations such as rotated, blurred, and high contrast versions of our images. This way, we had a total number of 375 images in our dataset. 
2.3.2 Model Architecture
We selected “YOLO11” from Ultralytics (specifically the small and nano variants) as our base detection model. This architecture provides an optimal balance between inference speed and detection accuracy. The nano version was trained first, and to enhance the performance, we switched to the small version called “YOLOv11s”. The model was trained on the custom Roboflow dataset, with an image size of 1280 and a total number of 100 epochs, resulting in a best.pt weight file capable of detecting cotton bolls with high confidence.
2.3.3 Training Performance Overview
The model was trained for 100 epochs, showing steady improvement in localization accuracy. The "Box Loss" graphs for both training and validation decreased consistently, indicating that the model learned to draw tighter and more accurate bounding boxes around the cotton bolls over time. The final Mean Average Precision (mAP 50-95) reached approximately 0.60, which represents a respectable baseline performance for a custom dataset in a real-world environment.

2.3.5 Classification Accuracy
According to the normalized confusion matrix, the model demonstrates excellent performance in detecting single cotton bolls, achieving a 95% accuracy rate. This high success rate confirms that the primary objective of identifying individual bolls has been met. The model rarely confuses single bolls with the background, proving that the visual features of the cotton are being correctly extracted by the YOLO architecture. Also it has a 82% accuracy rate on detecting unripe cotton bolls, with a limited quantity of augmented dataset.
